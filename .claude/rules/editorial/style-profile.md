# Style Profile

Extracted from analysis of recent posts (2026-01 to 2026-02). Complements the editorial rules (which say what NOT to do) with what the author actually does.

## Structure

### Opening pattern: concrete image first

Every strong post opens with a concrete image, anecdote, or scene before stating the thesis. The image carries the whole piece.

- "L'armure était la blessure. Il m'a fallu sept ans pour le voir."
- "Il y a une image dont je n'arrive pas à me défaire. Un éditeur de logiciels qui installe soigneusement un cadenas sur le portail de son jardin."
- "Years ago, I met a Z-System guru who was working for IBM during a mission at a bank."
- "There's one institution where the question of obedience was settled in blood: the army."

The image is never decorative. It becomes the conceptual framework for the entire argument. "The padlock without a wall" IS the thesis. "The armor was the wound" IS the thesis.

### Progression: image → mechanism → concrete case → implications → reframe

Typical movement:
1. Opening image/scene (1-2 paragraphs)
2. Mechanism explained ("Le mécanisme est simple.")
3. Concrete case from professional experience (Jolimoi, consulting missions, specific tools)
4. Broader implications (industry, society)
5. Closing that reframes the opening image or pushes the argument one step further

The conclusion never restates the intro. It adds a final twist or raises the stakes.

### Section breaks

Uses horizontal rules (`---`) between major sections in essay-style posts (no headings). Uses `##` headings in analytical/structured posts. Never mixes both in the same post.

## Paragraph & sentence patterns

### Paragraph length

Varies intentionally. Long analytical paragraphs (6-10 sentences) alternate with short punchy ones (1-2 sentences). The short paragraphs land key insights:

- "Sauf que le code est devenu une commodité."
- "Sauf que le code est devenu une commodité."
- "Both are honest."

### Sentence length

Mixes long compound sentences with short declarative ones. A single short sentence can land a key insight, but only after a long build-up (3+ sentences). Two consecutive short sentences is telegraphic and must be merged (see rhythm-and-flow.md). Binary reformulations ("X wasn't Y. It was Z.") must be joined with a semicolon or conjunction.

### Connective tissue

Ideas connect through conjunctions (mais, car, et, or, because, yet), semicolons, and relative clauses. Sentences within a paragraph link logically, not just topically. Characteristic connectors: "C'est pour ça que...", "Ce qui change, c'est...", "Le problème, c'est que...", "The point isn't that... It's that..."

## Voice

### First person: authority, not memoir

"Je" / "I" appears when:
- Stating a personal reading: "ma lecture de ce que l'essai ne dit pas", "From where I stand"
- Anchoring in professional experience: "chez Jolimoi", "during a mission at a bank"
- Expressing an opinion: "Je vois deux approches défensibles"

Never for emotional confession or hedging. The first person is a credential, not a confessional.

### Register

Conversational but precise. Technical terms used without explanation when the reader should know them (dbt, semantic layer, LLM, system prompt). Non-technical concepts explained through concrete images rather than definitions.

Tutoiement in French ("Le jour où tu tues l'ancien modèle"). Informal but not slangy.

### Rhetorical questions

Used sparingly and only when the question IS the argument:
- "Qui décide du comportement de l'agent, et avec quelle légitimité ?"
- "Quel est l'intérêt de verrouiller des fonctionnalités derrière un paywall quand le code sous-jacent n'a pas de valeur défensible ?"

Never used as filler or transition.

## Argumentation

### Single thesis, developed through examples

Each post has ONE central claim. Every section supports, illustrates, or extends it. No post tries to make three separate points.

### Examples: professional experience + historical/cultural references

Two types of examples coexist:
1. Direct professional experience (Jolimoi data team, IBM consultant, specific tools)
2. Historical/cultural analogies (US Constitution, Olympe de Gouges, Air France 447, photography vs painting)

The professional examples ground the argument. The cultural references elevate it. Both must be specific (names, dates, details), never generic.

### Intellectual honesty

- Acknowledges limits: "I don't have the answer."
- Acknowledges the other side: "She's probably better than no constitution at all."
- States uncertainty once and moves on. Never hedges the same point twice.

## Vocabulary

### Preferred patterns

- Metaphors from architecture, defense, and physical structures: armor, moat, wall, padlock, foundation, building
- Binary framings that reveal a paradox: "La complexité n'était pas de l'architecture, c'était le prix de la question."
- Reformulations that redefine the problem: "The double-run wasn't technical debt; it was a hedge against the meeting."

### What the author does NOT do

- No lists of three ("innovation, inspiration, insights")
- No superlatives for emphasis
- No performative conclusions ("the future is...")
- No hedging clusters ("could potentially possibly")
- No synonym cycling (reuses the clearest word)

## Bilingual patterns

Each version reads as if written natively. Key differences:
- French uses tutoiement and more direct phrasing
- English tends slightly more analytical, French slightly more conversational
- Cultural references may differ (French version might reference French law, English version American examples)
- The core argument and structure are identical; the surface language is adapted
