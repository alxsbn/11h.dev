# Writing Voice

The blog's tone is conversational and opinionated. Write like you're talking to a colleague over coffee, not presenting at a conference.

- Short sentences. Direct statements. Opinions stated plainly.
- Cite concrete facts, numbers, real examples rather than vague generalities.
- If something is uncertain, say so once and move on. Don't hedge every sentence.
- Reuse the clearest word rather than cycling through synonyms.

## Openings are concrete

Start every article with a fact, an image, or a tangible object. Never open with an abstraction or a grand statement. Lead with something the reader can see or touch, then narrow to the argument.

## One sustained metaphor per article

Each article is built around a single central metaphor developed across multiple sections (map/territory, mine/ore, manual/product). The metaphor is a vehicle for structural thinking, not decoration. Don't scatter unrelated metaphors.

## Structural contrasts

"It's not X, it's Y" inversions are a core argumentation tool. They reveal unexpected relationships and reframe assumptions. Use them to expose what the reader takes for granted, not just for rhetorical effect.

## Closings that complicate

Never summarize the intro in the conclusion. The final section must either sharpen the tension, reveal a hidden cost, or recontextualize the problem at a larger scale. If there's nothing new to add, end earlier.

## Personal voice for opinions

When expressing an opinion or interpretation, use first person. The blog is authored, not anonymous. "De mon point de vue", "ma lecture", "From where I stand", not detached third-person analysis. Use it sparingly (5-7 occurrences per ~3000-word article), reserved for moments of judgment or interpretation.

## Don't underestimate the reader

Choose examples that respect the reader's intelligence. If most people wouldn't actually do something (like asking a company AI agent how to negotiate their salary), don't use it as an example. Pick realistic scenarios.
